Daito-ryu Aikijujutsu, an historical outline (1)

Daito-ryu Aikijujutsu combat system was born around the year 1000, for military purpose, and right from its origin it has always been characterised by a variety of disciplines which were taught to the clan's ancient Japanese warriors. One of these disciplines, taijutsu , included these innovative and original technical concepts known as Aiki. In origin, in fact, the Daito school was known under the name of "Aiki-in-yo-ho" .
Aiki no jutsu (techniques using aiki) have a particular approach to an aggression, instead of using force to counter force or instead of yielding when faced with force (ju or yawara concepts), when applying aiki one "fuses" his vital energy with that of the opponent, and becomes one with it. The technical aspect is, however, to vast to be reduced in writing in a brief historical introduction to the art, and it may be item for successive study.
Aiki-in-yo-ho's taijutsu has undergone two principal areas of development before reaching us under the name of Daito-ryu Aikijujutsu.
These two areas were the Aiki philosophy (Aiki no jutsu) and the jujutsu philosophy. Aiki techniques became such a distinguishing and effective characteristic of the school that some centuries later in history (during the last 50 years or so) Daito's School Aiki no jutsu techniques became the core technical principles of other notable schools (Aikido, only to name the most renown).

But it was the tight and complete integration of these two philosophies that were to lead to the remarkable effectiveness and adaptability of the Art. Aiki no jutsu became one with other jujutsu techniques such as atemi , shime , kansetzu , kyushu , nage.
Since the very early ages the School taught all those military abilities which were essential to the Takeda army soldier: archery, sword, spear, halberd, horse riding, swimming, close quarter combat and many other disciplines.

Techniques were taught according to whom they were destined to: foot troops (ashigaru ), low ranking bushi (samurai ), horse mounted bushi, captains, and commanders.The highest-ranking officers were also instructed in military strategy, calligraphy, poetry, classical readings, and formal rules of behaviour to be kept inside the daymio's palace.

But all bushi begun their military formation very early, often as early as 10 years old. They entered the training dojos to practice for the rest of their lives those techniques which would have served them in battle, and all bushi were brought up with only one value and one law, the law of the feudal Japanese warrior, the "bushido" .

The genesis of this martial art and its evolution is to be dated back to feudal times. It has reached our imagination through literature, romance, and history, shaping for us that vivid idea of the Japanese warrior, fearless and proud. The Takeda clan has engraved its name with honour in all major Japanese military events so much so that often historical periods of that country are marked by events that concearn the clan. The Daito School thus ploughed its way through history unscathed by time, revolutions, wars, social unrest, and duels to see the 3rd Millennium dawn. Starting from 1087, year of its foundation, the clan has always been led by a member of the Takeda family until 1992, when its last soke (Takeda Tokimune) died without a heir. His most senior and expert pupils, some having been uchi-deshi , wanted to preserve the original techniques exactly as they had been handed down to them, and in order to keep alive the Art founded the Daito-ryu Aikijujutsu. But let's visit the origins of the School and the Takeda clan.

Oral tradition has it that it was Shinra Saburo Yoshimitsu Minamoto (1057-1127), third son of Minamoto Yoriyoshi , fifth generation descendant of Japan's emperor Fujiwara Seiwa (member of the Minamoto dynasty), to create the art. And this tradition is supported by historical evidence and collateral documents in possession of the Takeda family today.
Yoshimitsu had been instructed in a martial art developed by his family generations before him, and it is likely that the original source was the Tegoi . The Tegoi was a native form of unarmed combat from which it is said to have been derived also Sumo wrestling.
These techniques were handed down in the strictest secret only to family members who would teach them to their clan's warriors; this tradition has been respected up until mid 20th century.

Historians divide Japan's history in eras and periods after the foundation of the first stable capital, Nara, these periods are:
Nara: (710-794); Heian (up to 1195) which was characterized by the rise to power of the imperial court; Kamakura (until 1333), which introduces the military leadership of the Shogun ; Ashikaga (until al 1392); Muromachi (until 1572); Momoyama (until 1603, this date is controversial: some hold 1600 others 1615). These last 3 periods saw continuous internal wars in the struggle for power and leadership. Then the Edo period dawns (ending in 1867), also known as "Pax Tokugawa". The fall of the shogun and the restoration of the emperor's power and court leads to the Meiji period (until 1912). In this time Japan begins its industrial and social revolution which leads us into modern times.

The name Takeda echoes in Japan's history hall and the epic feats of the members of this clan are recorded in the history books, as a perfect example of the resolute and fearless character of those times' bushi. The clan's battles and episodes are to be found tightly woven into the military and political threads of the country. What follows is a short description of the most notorious events in history tied to the clan's name.

The Minamoto clan was amongst the most eminent ones in feudal Japan, and prince Minamoto Yoriyoshi , lord of the province of Chinjufu had been commanded by the emperor to quench Abe's clan revolt.
But it took him 11 years (1051-1062) before he could rise his ensign on Sadatou Abe's castle after the battle of Yakata Koromogawa.

His sons later fought in the Gosannen war (1083-1087), again on imperial mandate, against the Kiyohara clan. Minamoto Yoshiie (one of these sons) was almost overwhelmed when Yoshimitsu, his brother, joined forces with him, and together they seiged Kanazawa castle to be met with success one year later.
These two brothers have left a sinister memory, as it is told that their ruthless and ferocious methods were equalled by their scientific interest in human anathomy.
They sectioned the bodies of dead enemies in order to study the structure of joints, muscles, nerves and bones so as to transfer their knowledge in the family's unarmed combat techniques of which Yoshimitsu was master.

Yoshimitsu was the lord of the castle of Daito (which will later gave its name to this martial art), but his son Yoshikiyo at the end of the war moved away from his father's domain to the province known as Kai where he took upon himself the name of Takeda Yoshikiyo .

Document evidence proves that since when Minamoto Yoshikiyo had moved to that province (Kai, next to nowadays Tokyo, in 1087), the new clan adopted the name of Kai genji Takeda. (Heian period 794 - 1195). Thus was born the Takeda clan, and Yoshikiyo, in recognition of his father's services, one year later was created hereditary military prince by the emperor with the concession of lands and rights to collect taxes and conscript an army.

Though some dispute the origins of the clan ante 1087 claiming that no proven evidence is today available to avail this lineage, it must be noted that independent historical literature tells us about the Minamoto clan's feats and Yoshikiyo's decision to move to the province of Kai. Further, other documents, state that a renown feudal lord's son, was in that time created prince in recognition of his fathers' help to the emperor's cause. Further independent sources agree that the clan's origin are to be placed within those of one of the most senior clans of the times, without, alas, stating its name.

Last, it is worth of notice that the Minamoto's clan members Yoshokyio, his father Yoshimitsu, his uncle Yoshiie, and his grandfather Yoriyoshi, are characters repeatedly named in Japanese Heian period literature recalling those very same battles and events.

The genealogical correlation is thus, not only possible, but in fact that most likely.
After 1087 we cross many oral and written sources that greatly help to reconstruct the clan's history and the evolution of its own martial art. Besides the battle for the Kanazawa castle (above) 14th November 1087 (Heian period, 794 - 1195) we are told of the famous daymio Takeda Shingen (1570-1572, Muromachi period 1392-1572). He fought at the service of the Ashikaga shogun in many military campaigns and wars (for example against Kenshin Uesugi, daymio of Echigo) without ever being defeated until he was treacherously assassinated while travelling to the capital.
With his death most historians close the Muromachi period as it marks the point of decline of the Ashikaga dominion and the rise of the new shogun clan, the Tokugawa.

An epic event in Japan's history is the battle of Nagashino no Kassen in may 21st, 1575 (Momoyama period, 1572 - 1603). In this battle the main looser was "bushido" itself. In fact it was openly violated for the first time in history marking the end of an era, when the combined armies of Oda Nobunaga and Yeiyasu Tokugawa, many times greater in number, deployed firearms against the enemy. 15.000 horsemen, the pride of the Takeda clan, the famous and undefeated Takeda heavy cavalry, were completely slaughtered.

These events are told with the necessary adaptation in the film "Kagemusha" (The shadow of the warrior) by Akira Kurosawa, who later used more ideas from the Takeda saga to direct other notable films.

The invasion of the province of Kai marks the fall of the last stronghold of the Kai-Takeda clan in the spring of 1582, when the combined armies of Nobunaga and Tokugawa defeated the army of the clan. Daymio Takeda Katsuyori ( 1534 -1582)chose ritual suicide - seppuku - with all his family.

Takeda Shingen, in his last will, aware of the decline of the Ashikaga dynasty had that his nephew Takeda Kunitsugu should move to the province of Aizu where was lord daymio Moriuji Ashina (still faithfull to him and to the Ashikaga shogun).
Daymio Moriuji Ashina in respect of his old friend gave Kunitsugu some land and a castle and it would be the latter, excellent master of the family martial art, to continue the name and tradition of the clan and the School.

Defeated in its military prowess but not in its spirit the clan slides into a century long, self imposed, confinement: it had been the leading clan which fought for the Ashikaga dynasty against the new all powerful shogun…!

From this point onwards the Takeda's only disclosed the school's teachings to the senior dignitaries of the Aizu clan in recognition of their help. So the tie between the two clans became stronger every day that passed. Soon that branch of the clan became known as clan Aizu-Takeda (to differentiate from the Kai-Takeda branch).

In 1600, at Sekigahara, Ieyasu Tokugawa defeated Hideyosi and opened a new period in history, known as Edo period (1603-1867) sometimes called Tokugawa period, after the name of the winning clan. During this 250 years long period the country would have enjoyed a relatively peaceful period, a peace imposed with force by the new shogun.
One of the first edits of his bakufu was the reconciliation with the defeated clans of the Momoyama period (1572-1603) and the introduction of the rigid social class structure which so much became characteristic of the period (1603). Soon followed strict rules that in fact segregated the country in a long period of isolation from the rest of the world (1604).

Political rehabilitation was at hand for the Takeda clan, that could now join the new history course, and in 1664, almost a century after their military defeat, they could openly teach again their complete combat system with all its battle techniques. It is then that, within the territory of Mutsu, we find records of the Nishinkan dojo where the clan had continued to forge bushi in the secrecy of its confinement, and we now have descriptions of its activities. The aura of myth which will soon envelop the Nishinkan dojo was beginning to shine…

Then, in 1674, document evidence shows that the Takeda's dojo, the Nishinkan, had spread its influence to cover a large number of dojos that taught only to the Aizu clan's bushi.
Amongst the main stream schools there were:
5 Sabre and 2 jujutsu schools (amongst them the renown Mizu- no-Shinto-ryu and Shinmyo-ryu).
And a multitude of private dojos which would train the same bushi of the Aizu clan:
22 sabre schools ( kenjutsu), 16 jujutsu schools, 16 firearms schools, 14 sabre extraction (iaijutsu or battojutsu) schools, 7 archery schools (kyujutsu), 4 spear schools and one halberd school ( sojutsu and naginatajutsu).
Among these schools two gave instruction only to high dignitaries of the Aizu clan, and that instruction was given in the strictest secrecy: no one was allowed to approach the two dojos.
These were clan Aizu's pride; they were the Takeda school of Oshiki uchi (already known as Aiki-in-yo-ho in earlier times and later known as Daito-ryu Aikijujutsu) and the Misoguchi-ha-itto-ryu kenjutsu of Clan Aizu.

Then, as a final seal visible to all, came the shogun's request for the clan to train and forge his own personal guards. It was the clan's apex in fame and importance: technical skills and abilities had to be beyond doubt and utmost to win that high preference.
The Takeda were again the foremost technical experts of military skills, and their services became requested throughout the capital and the country.

However, 250 years of peace and isolation from the rest of the world had taken their toll and had weakened the buke class more than a long war. The buke class had lost its edge and had begun a slow and inevitable decline.
But the luxurious and decadent life of the capital, was very distant from the western clans every day life: the privileges of the Edo dignitaries and lords irritated these distant daymios who envisaged the unbalance in wealth and privileges as open disdain for them.

These circumstances favoured the conditions for the outburst of the Boshin war in 1868 that was to end the Edo period of military leadership of the country. Once more loyal to the constituted authority the Takeda clan, like many of the most important clans, fought to defend the shogun under the leadership of their brother clan Aizu.
Against them were ready to wage war and move towards Edo the western clans guided by those of Satsuma and Choshu, with the intent to restore the emperor's Meiji dynasty to power, who, in turn, had promised land and more privileges to these.

During the Edo period the town of Aizu-Wakamatsu, in the province of Aizu, was famous for the strength of Tsurugajo castle, which had been built in 1384 (Ashikaga period 1333-1392) by Ashina Naomori (at the time Aizu's daymio). When the Boshin war broke out it was garrisoned by Aizu troops trained by the Takeda Clan, that also supplied the bushi for the shoguns personal guard..
