FINAL ON SAMURAI
 
 
 THE WAY OF THE SAMUARI

  A samurai was the hereditary  warrior class in feudal Japan.  The term originally referred only to the imperial guards. After the coming of the feudal system, it meant the entire military class.  This included the samurai warriors, the feudal lords called daimyos, and the shogun.  About 5 of every 100 Japanese belonged to this group.

                     A code of unquestioning obedience and loyalty, called Bushido, bound the Samurai warriors  to their lords.  The Samurai prized honor above wealth of life and atoned for dishonor by committing  hara-kiri, or ceremonial suicide.  They wore two swords and a distinctive headdress.  The Samurai were graded in military ranks, each with an appropriate income in rice. They lost their privileges when the Japanese abolished feudalism in 1871.
 
                      One who is a Samurai must before all things keep constantly in mind, by day and night,
from the morning when he takes up his chopsticks to eat his New Year's breakfast to Old Year's
night when he pays his yearly bills, the fact that he has to die.   If he is mindful of this, he will be able to
live in accordance with the paths of Loyalty and Filial Duty, will avoid myriad's of evil and adversities,
keep himself free from disease and calamity and more over enjoy a long life.  I am afraid that in this
paper I only scratch the surface, I can only relate the information and ask you to discover the depth
at which these codes mean to those whom follow them.
 

 Filial Duty.

                       One who is a Samurai should base his conduct on a strong sense of Filial duty.  Regard-
less if one is capable, clever, eloquent and handsome, if he is unfilial he is of no use at all.   Bushido
requires a man's conduct to be correct in all points and have a knowledge of what's right.  One who
doesn't know this cannot be a Samurai and once insight is gained, one sees the parents as authors
of their beings.  Now when rendering filial obligations to parents, there are two variables.  The first
is a parent who is honest, educates his children with sincere kindliness and leaves them all his
property.  This consists of income above average, weapons, horse furniture, household treasures,
as well as arranging good marriages for them.  When the parent retires, one must care and meet
the every need of the retiree.  This is what is meant by filial duty.  The other variable is a crude and
crotchety parent who nags, insists that all property is his, gives his children nothing and despite
scanty means of the family, always asks for drink, food and clothes.   Even such a cantankerous
parent must be reverenced as a parent and bad attitude must be endured without showing signs of
annoyance.  Exerting oneself to the utmost for a parent is real filial piety.

                                    "It is forbidden to forget the great debt of kindness
                                     one owes to his master and ancestors
                                    and thereby make light of the virtues of loyalty and filial piety."
                                                                                                     "The Regulations of Imagawa Ryoshun"

                           A Samurai who possesses this spirit when he enters the service of a lord will
thoroughly understand the Way of Loyalty and will show it not only when his master is prosperous
but also if he meets adversary.  Filial duty is vital to a Samurai and terms like "parent" and "lord,"
"Filial Conduct" and "Loyalty" are distinct and yet they are no different in meaning.  If one cannot be
Filial to his parents, it is unlikely that he will give loyal service to a lord of no relation out of pure
gratitude.  If he cannot perform then he is useless.


 
 
 Samurai Ordinances.

                            In Bushido there are two ordinances and four sections.  The two ordinances consist
of ordinary and extraordinary.  Ordinary is divided into two sections of the official and of the
soldiers, while the extraordinary is divided into army and of battle affairs.  In the section of the
Samurai officials, they must wash their hands and feet every night and morning and take a hot bath
to keep themselves clean.   A Samurai must do his hair every morning and keep the hair properly
shaved from his forehead.  The he must always wear the ceremonial dress proper to the occasion
and  of course wear his two swords as well as carry a fan in his girdle.  When he receives a guest
he must treat him with the etiquette due to his rank and must refrain from idle talk.  When not
serving he must not lounge about aimlessly but read and practice writing.  By doing this he stores
his mind with ancient history and precepts of the warrior houses and conducts himself at all times
so that his manners are those proper to a Samurai.  The section on soldiers consists of exercises
like fencing, spear practicing, horsemanship and shooting a bow; a Samurai is an official for extra-
ordinary conditions and when the country is in a state of disorder, he will leave from his daily life
to serve under his lord as commander.  They then put their armor on and advance into enemy
territory, this is a matter of the army affairs.   Then comes the rule of battle affairs, which is the method of handling the army when it comes into contact with the enemy to give battle.  To be called a first-class  Samurai is to be skilled in all four sections of these two ordinances.  To have knowledge
of only two  sections may be fit for a average cavalier, but no one ignorant of the extraordinary sections can become a commander or high officier such as monogashirsa or bugyo.  This is an
important consideration a Samurai must make to realize that they cannot rise in position without profound study in the extraordinary ordinance.
 
 

The Offensive Spirit.

                                It is important that a Samurai never forget his offensive spirit at any time.  With
death firmly fixed in the mind, a Samurai must anticipate anything and maintain an aggressive spirit.
 
                                           " When you leave your gate, act as though an
                                                   enemy was in sight."

                                It was a belief that one, even being a farmer, merchant and artisan, should cherish
some rusty blade.  Even wear a blunt sword or wooden one when they went to bath.  Despite not
having an aggressive spirit, one who wears a sword is nothing but a farmer or a tradesman in a warrior's skin.
 

Right and Wrong.

                                    A warrior should have a thorough understanding of these two qualities.  If he
knows how to do one and avoid the other then he will have attained Bushido.  To be unstable and
make no distinction between right and wrong is contrary to reason, anyone who understands this distinction  and yet does what is wrong is no proper Samurai, but a raw and untaught person.
And the cause of it is small capacity for self-control, if we examine into its origin we find it arises
from cowardice.  That is why a  Samurai must refrain from wrong and cleave to what is right.

                                       Generally the rule of practice of right conduct is to feel shame at the contempt
of our family, servants and friends.  Then at the scorn of the wider circle of our acquaintance and
of outsiders, and eschew the wrong and do the right.  In time this becomes a habit and one will prefer the right over the wrong.
 
 

Bravery
 

                                           The three qualities of Loyalty, Right Conduct and Bravery are essential
for Bushido.  The three qualities are loyal warrior, righteous warrior and the valiant warrior.
The Samurai who possesses these, only he will be a warrior of the highest class.  Among the
myriad's of Samurai, a loyal and righteous warrior maybe easy to find, but times of peace a brave
warrior is hard to single out.  Bravery is not present when a Samurai puts his armor one, you
will see this by his loyalty in his lord and parents.  Among other characteristics , he will not
neglect study and military arts;  He will be reluctant to squander his money or useless trivial
wants and needs.  It is rigid self-control that is the beginning of Valor.
 
 


 
 
 
 
 
 Respect.
 

                                    In Bushido, however loyal and filial a man may be in his heart, if he lacks the
proper etiquette and manners which displays respect to  lord or parent, he cannot be regarded
as living in confromity with it.  A man like this can be no proper Samurai.  Even in private or public,
one is not to disregard respect to lord or parent.
 

Duties of a Samurai.
 
                            "The field of battle is my normal abode."
                                                              Anonymous Samurai
 
                    The duties of a Samurai are two fold, military and constructive.  When at war
he must be at camp always alert regardless of day or night.  Construction is associated here,
all sorts of strongholds, moats, embattled camps and fortified outposts have to be up in little
time as possible.  In time of peace they are allowed to have such duties as guards, escorts,
inspectors, and come to see the stay-at-home function as normal and think of field service like
a dream.  When the time comes that they are asked to assist in the shogun's government, they
gripe and rumble not realizing that to take part in both military and constructive activities are
the regular business of a Samurai.  Some find it quite hard even in times of peace, however they
find it just as hard when they are asked to travel.  When they are sent out as Traveling Inspector,
they resent the fatigue and expense taken.  Despite the fact that it maybe near, they complain
of how many trips they make or the state of the weather.  Those that fulfil their duty in a mean
spirit are nothing more than low down servants in the skins of Samurai.   Samurai's true to the
Bushido will not take this as a burden or hardship, but all in the ordinary day's work.
 
 

 


 

                                        As stated in before I introduced some ideas and I hope I provoked an interest.
To end this I thought about writing some huge reflection on my recent study, while looking for
information I found this interesting quote that wraps it up great.   This was written in the seventeeth
century by a Samurai who had become a Zen Buddhist Monk.

       "The Way of the Samurai is found in death. When it comes to either /or, there is only the quick
choice of  death. When pressed with the choice of life or death, it is not necessary to gain one's aim.
We all want to live. And in large part we make our logic according to what we like. But not having attained our aim and continuing to live is cowardice. This is a dangerous thin line. To die without
gaining one's aim is a dog's death and fanaticism. But there is no shame in this. This is the substance
of the Way of the Samurai. If by setting one's heart right every morning and evening, one is able to
live as though his body were already dead, he gains freedom in the Way. His whole life will be without blame, and he will succeed in his calling.... Being a retainer is nothing other than being a supporter of one's lord, entrusting matters of good  and evil to him, and renouncing self-interest. If there are
but two or three men of this type, the fief will be secure. Loyalty is said to be important in the pledge between lord and retainer. Though it may seem unobtainable, it is right before your eyes. If you
once set yourself to it, you will become a superb retainer at that very moment.... The person without previous resolution to the inevitable death makes certain that his death will be in bad form. But if one
is resolved to death beforehand, in what way can he be despicable? One should be especially diligent in this concern. If one were to say a word what the condition of being a samurai is, its basis lies first in seriously devoting one's body and soul to his master. And if one is asked what to do beyond this, it
would be to fit oneself inwardly with intelligence, humanity, and courage. The combining of these
three  virtues may seem unobtainable to the ordinary person, but it is easy. Intelligence is nothing
more than discussing things with others. Limitless wisdom comes from this. Humanity is something
done for the sake of others, simply comparing oneself with them and putting them in the fore. Courage
is gritting one's teeth; it is simply doing that and pushing ahead, paying no attention to   the circumstances. Anything that seems above these three is not necessary to be known. As for outward aspects, there are personal appearance, one's way of speaking and calligraphy. And as all of these are daily matters, they improve by constant practice. Basically, one should perceive  their nature to be
one of quite strength. If one has accomplished all these things, then he should have a knowledge of our area's history and customs. After that he may study the various arts as recreation. If you think it
over, being a retainer is simple. And these days, if you observe people who are even a bit useful,
you will see that they have accomplished these three outward aspects.
