The Samurai's True Face
An Essay on the Attitudes and
Motivations of the Classic Samurai

by Geordie Keefe

Introduction

The samurai has long been a subject of fascination to westerners, but has been at least as misunderstood as the pop-culture spawning ninja. Samurai were never mindless automatons or honor-crazed warriors. Their environment shaped the behavior that seems so foreign to many, but did not destroy the individuality within. The legendary devotion to honor and disdain of life of the samurai were only reactions (often distorted by popular legend) to the dangerous lives they led. What truly motivated the samurai can not be learned from those reactions alone, but from a multitude of ambitions and feelings, some of which I hope to unveil.

Herein I hope to show the actual motivations and emotions of the samurai. With samurai history largely affected by legend and romantic exaggeration, some of my interpretations may be more outrageous or conservative than those that would be made by a professional scholar on the matter. I hope for the reader's patience and appreciation of the difficulty of the subject.

I do not intend to expound any tremendous revelation on the nature of the samurai in this essay. Only to exorcise any opinions about the samurai which I hold to be common misconceptions. Samurai (from the Japanese saburo - to serve) being a fairly vague term, for these purposes I will mainly use it in reference to any member of Japan's elite warrior class, predominantly from around the tenth to seventeenth century.

A Brief History

To lay a foundation for the samurai culture requires some knowledge of their background and environment. Though impossible to go into depth on such a subject here, I will breifly cover Japanese history in the time of the samurai, touching on some of the more significant events for this essay.

Professional warriors have existed in Japan almost as long as people have existed there; practically necessitated by the inhospitable nature of Japan. Supplementing this, peasant conscription (taken from the Chinese practice) was quite common by the third century. At the end of the eighth century, such conscription was halted in favor of a permanent professional military, taken from the sons and brothers of district magistrates, and called kondei. Large military forces were mostly unnecessary, as Japan had little contact with hostile neighbors (excepting the Emishi and the Mongols, discussed below), and large armies were prone to poor discipline and suffering from (as well as causing) famine. The kondei showed many signs of being the progenitors of the samurai, being an elite fighting group, mostly specialized in mounted archery. Contact with the Emishi brought them even closer to the familiar model of the samurai.

Througout the eighth century the Imperial Court conducted a campaign to conquer the 'barbarians' of northern Japan, called Emishi. Though their customs were different, there are some reasons to believe the Emishi were physically similar (if not identical) to the Japanese. Success in this unprovoked invasion rose and fell; the Japanese had greater numbers, but the excellent archery and guerrilla warfare of the Emishi evened the odss. Their techniques were so effective that the Japanese adopted the one-edged sword and light armor of the Emishi, and possibly some archery techniques. This era also saw the origin of Japan's highest military rank, the Sei-i-tai shogun ('Great Barbarian-Subduing Generalissimo'), bound to play a pivitol role later in Japan's history. Development of the crossbow turned the tide against the Emishi, who generally conceded integration into Japan.

From the Kanto area where the campaign against the Emishi had taken place came many of Japan's most influencial warriors. Minamoto Yoshitomo came from here, and used the Kanto area as a power base in his conflict against the Imperial court's most influential followers, the Taira. Where Yoshitomo failed, his son Toritomo succeeded. After defeating the Taira in 1185, Yoritomo took the reigns of power from the court, and later had them pronounce him Sei-i-tai shogun. Increases in productivity (and subsequently an excess of goods) in recent centuries had laid the foundation for the samurai, and Yoritomo cemented it. Samurai became a wealthy landowning class, answerable to a chain of superiors that led to Yoritomo's Kamakura shogunate, not the Emperor.

In 1274 Japan faced its first major outside threat, an invading fleet from the Mongol empire. Though skilled fighters, the Japanese samurai were accustomed to an almost ritualistic form of battle; pronouncing their lineage and accomplishments to the enemy, in hope of drawing out a worthy opponent for single combat. The well-organized Mongol invaders took the advantage, but stubborn resistance and sea storms forced their withdrawal. Seven years later they returned with an even greater force, and though better prepared this time there was little the Japanese forces could do to stop them. By sheer luck (attributed to divine intervention), storms rose that devastated the Mongol fleet. To defend Japan against future incursions, military strength was increased and the samurai gained rights to raise troops and provisions from temple and aristocratic estates. Samurai on the whole reached a new pinnacle of power, but not so the shogunate. Since the defending samurai gained little loot and no land from the invading Mongols, they looked to the shogunate to compensate them. Attempts to do so weakened the Kamakura shogunate, and failures to do so drained support from it, until finally it was overthrown by the Ashikaga family.

Though the Ashikaga were not as powerful as the Minamoto, Japan did enjoy almost a century of relative peace under them. This ended with the Onin war, lasting from 1467-1477, when central power in Japan was effectively destroyed. Following this was a century of mayhem and warfare, as regional samurai rose as Daimyo ('big names', comparable to feudal lords), and seized all they could. Though samurai remained the ruling class, this time also saw a return of conscription, and with it the large, unruly armies of Japan's earlier days.

Bloody though it may have been, this time produced some of the finest warriors and military minds in Japanese history. In particular Oda Nobunaga, who unified Japan again; his successor Hideyoshi; and finally Tokugawa Ieyasu. Though all were excellent military leaders, only Ieyasu managed to establish a dynasty and have himself proclaimed shogun. Under the Tokugawa shogunate followed a two and a half century reign of peace. Samurai turned from warcraft to bureacracy, retaining only the image of their former ways. When the Meiji restoration came in 1868, most samurai were prepared to abandon even that, ending their long rule over Japan.

Misconceptions

Two of the main misconceptions about samurai are their fanatical loyalty and their total lack of fear of death. Both have basis in fact, but can be refuted. For starters, fanatical loyalty was largely based on self-interest.

What was probably the greatest influencer of samurai was a cross between reputation and honor; often referred to as na (name). More than just integrity, it was the foundation for the life of a samurai. Without a dignified reputation, a samurai was nothing. Loyal adherance to a single lord was often motivated by such a self-interest. Improving one's reputation would improve one's standing, lands, and other benefits; cowardice or disloyalty could have them taken away, and a samurai uprooted for such reasons would often die in misery and poverty. Betrayal, however, could set one higher than before in a rival camp; a tarnish to honor, perhaps, but an improvement in status. Some scholars have suggested that a sense of duty was completely secondary to honor and reputation. When Saeki Tsunenori charged to his death supporting Minamoto Yoriyoshi, he was followed by several of his vassals. Faced with being condemned for cowardice, they chose to protect their honor and reputation, even to the death.

Charging boldly into death is another common attribute of the romantic samurai. Though such cases appear to have been over-exaggerated, and retreat and surrender under-exaggerated, the samurai definitely did hold a fearless attitude toward death. On the other hand, often when they held such an attitude it was because the alternative was death anyway, in addition to a loss to one's na. No discussion of the samurai and death can be complete without a reference to seppuku (ritual suicide); a sure sign of the fearlessness of the samurai, but still adhering to the reasoning I've mentioned. Two predominant reasons for committing seppuku were to avoid capture and to atone for criminal acts. Since capture generally meant death, preceded by torture and humiliation, suicide was considered a far better alternative. This was not unique to Japan; it was also a fairly common alternative in ancient Rome and other places. Similarly, certain crimes held a death sentence, but a samurai could choose to take his own life, avoiding damage to the family reputation and the humiliation of a public hanging. Though initially an excrutiatingly painful act, as seppuku grew 'in vogue', it became sufficient for the samurai only to show that he was resolved to die. As soon as he had picked up his sword or touched it to his belly, his kaishakunin (second) would cut his head off. At one point, even the sword was abandoned; the samurai would take a symbolic item (often a fan) to show his resolve, whereupon his second would commit the actual dirty work.

One flaw in my argument is in the act of committing seppuku to follow a lord or master into death. Though understandable in defeat, this act became exceedingly common in the peaceful years of the Kamakura shogunate. Perhaps it was a sign that the samurai honor was not as selfish as some scholars propose. Still, after a while it became so common that it was damaging to the reputation not to do so. Undeterred at first by the disapproval of the shogun, this practice was finally put to an end when the shogunate made good on its threats to severly punish the heirs of all involved.

Bravery in the face of death may also have led some to wrongly perceive that the samurai gave no value to life. An often exaggerated fact is that samurai had the ability to kill any commoner they wished for the slightest offense. This may have been true, but actual cases of doing so are infrequent. Samurai could not afford to hurt their name with a reputation for killing needlessly. Buddhist values and dependence on the common people also made such acts unnacceptable, though the predominant reason may have been the simplest: commoners did not often give the samurai reason to take offense.

Ideals

Any condemnation I make of the samurai should not be taken as a sign of any moral bankruptcy on their part. On the contrary, lapses in their morals are quite understandable, as the samurai operated under a heavy load of ideals. The samurai were encouraged to follow Buddhism, despite their bloody business and the threats of hell that followed from it. They were expected to show total devotion to tehri lord and to their parents, with the occasional risk that they would be forced to choose one side over the other. It wasn't for nothing that people compared duty to a mountain!

The main ideals of the samurai can best be found (in my opinion) in Yamamoto Tsunetomo's Hagakure. It is an inspiring, straight-forward book, that returns often to the ideal Yamamoto cites; "The way of the samurai is death". However, the book should be taken with a grain of salt, as its writer was never actually in combat. Yamamoto (like so many others) greatly romanticizes the selfless loyalty and honor of the samurai. Undoubtably, so many do so because writing otherwise would make for an unappealing book. Still, whether selfish or not, one of the great ideals of the samurai has always been to face death without fear. To do otherwise would be a great disgrace to his reputation.

The value of that reputation for a samurai can not be overemphasized. It was by ones reputation that one achieved rank and the land attached to it, and by land that one supported the vassals that were the foundation of power. For the most part, samurai were judged by birth, ability, and reputation; after the Onin wars, birth became less important, allowing Hideyori and others to rise out of nothing to the top. Ambition to improve one's reputation was possibly the greatest driving factor in the samurai's history. That ambition was another burden to the samurai, for while pride was acceptable, ambition was stifled (quite naturally) from above. The value of this reputation was so important that many show surprise at the rapid collapse of the samurai during the Meiji restoration. At this point, reputation was waning in value, but the ambition that drove it was still there. They felt that well educated and aggressive samurai could feed their ambitions better if free to pursue the profit-making formerly denied to them. Some were proved wrong, but others were right.

In behavior, the samurai were expected to behave much like the classic knights of King Arthur: respectful, dignified, compassionate, gallant, truthful, etc. The reality was often otherwise, as shown in the acts of Miyamoto Musashi. Often rude and arrogant, Musashi was noted for not even having the grace to bathe regularly. This, however, may have been a response to his having been ambushed twice in a bath; once being captured, the second time fighting his way out. Musashi exemplified the difference between an ideal samurai and a successful one; he kept himself alive.

The True Face

In many noble acts, the samurai acted in self-interest, but this does not mean that everything they did was selfish. While many samurai of his age were turning to bureacracy, Musashi followed a life dedicated to sword-fighting. This is not flawlessly noble, however; sword-training was still a lucrative trade at the time, and it should be remembered that Musashi was notoriously eccentric. Nobility was often shown in filial devotion as well, though this too was often distorted to selfish ends. Families would sometimes take both sides of a war, father on one side, oldest son on the other, to ensure success for the family (and hopefully a pardon for the loser). However, many instances of sacrifice and devotion appear to have been from honest love. The samurai were much like the people of other lands and times; some were selfish, some were not.

Recommended Reading

Daily Life in Japan at the Time of the Samurai (1185-1603) - Louis Frederic. A good book regarding everyday life. I agree that the samurai had the greatest effect on the culture of Japan during their time, but Frederic is a bit overly demeaning of the lifestyles and contributions of the lower classes.

A Book of Five Rings - Miyamoto Musashi.

The Nobility of Failure; Tragic Heroes in the History of Japan - Ivan Morris. A very moving book, the tragic aspect may be a bit played up for his purposes.

Told Round a Brushwood Fire - Arai Hakuseki. The autobiography of a very serious samurai; becomes quite dry after the first part.

Hagakure - Yamamoto Tsunetomo. Tied with A Book of Five Rings for the greatest book on samurai philosophy. Yamamoto tells several small stories and anecdotes to keep the book interesting.

Miyamoto Musashi - Eiji Yoshikawa. Not very appropriate for study as it is a fiction, but definitely an excellent book.

Musui's Story - Katsu Kokichi. The autobiography of Katsu Kokichi. Almost a parody of Told Round a Brushwood Fire, Kokichi lead a fun, shameless life.

Legends of the Samurai - Hiroaki Sato. A compilation of many of the best samurai stories and fables.

Bibliography

Hagakure - Yamamoto Tsunetomo, translated by William S. Wilson, Kodansha International Ltd., 1980. (2nd ed.)
Musui's Story - Katsu Kokichi, translated by Teruko Craig, The University of Arizona Press, 1988.
Heavenly Warriors - William W. Farris, Harvard University Press, 1992.
Feudalism in Japan - Peter Duus, McGraw-Hill Inc., 1993 (3rd ed.)
Hired Swords - Karl F. Friday, Stanford University Press, 1994.
The Taming of the Samurai - Eiko Ikegami, Harvard University Press, 1995.
