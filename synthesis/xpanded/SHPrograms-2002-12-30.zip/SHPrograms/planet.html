introduction

Since the discovery of the first extrasolar planet by Queloz and Mayor in 1995, theorists get more and more data about the properties of extrasolar planets and their systems.
Our solar system is not anymore the only one existing, it is not anymore special. Most of the new found extrasolar planets have extreme properties: Extrasolar planets were found with masses up to 15 Jupitermasses, semi-major axis down to 0.04 AU and eccentricities up to 0.93.
So far, all extrasolar planets have been detected using the high-precision radial velocity (Doppler) measurements, two of them with a direct transit of the planet in front of the central star. While there are a lot of new ideas for the detection, both directly using infrared techniques and indirectly by microlensing effects, no extra solar planet could be found by any different method. Planets with significantly lower mass than Jupiter cannot be detected with current radial velocity and moreover planets with a large semi-major axis and therefore long periods require a long time measurement of several years. That is why less massive planets on greater orbital radii are expected to be found in the next years. There is, however, evidence for two sub-saturn massive planets.
Based on present knowledge, it is assumed that about 5% of solar-type stars host massive planets, which means in other words the number of planets in our galaxy can be up to one billion.

 

 

theory

 

 

 

 

 

 

Planet formation is highly correlated to star formation. Today theories assume planet formation and star formation to take place in the same protoplanetary or protostellar accretion disk. So let us first take a look at star formation itself:

In the so-called standard model of star formation, stars form from gravitational instabilities in interstellar clouds of dust and gas. If in a certain region of the cloud the gravitational binding energy exceeds its thermal energy, the region begin to contract. This is called the Jeans instability criterion. This can happen due to a distortion by e.g. a shock wave due to a novae nearby. After several time, a accretion disk is formed out of the cloud due to angular momentum conservation. Dust and grain particles now settle to the midplane in the disk.

These accretion disks are the birthplaces of planets. In a first step, dust and grain particles hit and stick together in the disk and form objects with sizes up to several meters, all of them orbiting the central star in the same direction. As a comparable picture, one might think about the objects found in the rings of Saturn. Further collisions lead to the formation of so-called planetesimals with sizes up to one kilometer that interact by gravitation. Afterwards, the gravitational interaction leads to changes in their orbits and growing is possible by further collisions. Finally one gets so-called embryos, that can have masses of about 10e-7 solar masses in the terrestrial region. Out of these embryos form the later planets, depending on several circumstances either terrestrial planets or giant planets are formed. Terrestrial planets consist of rock material while giant planets are gas planets, which can only form beyond the so-called snowy line, the ice-condensation radius, which is about 4 AU. Fragmentation and collisions lead to the formation of terrestrial planets, while giant gas planets form differently:
Following the so-called core instability model, a massive solid core located beyond the ice-condensation radius can get large enough to accrete gas out of the disk, and finally gets more and more massive.
As a result of all these accretion and collision processes, there are a lot of less massive rock particles at arbitrary orbits, which form the later comets.
Finally the planet formation process stops by the disappearence of the accretion disk itself.

 

 

problems

 

 

 

 

 

 

Based on the new data of extrasolar planets, one gets into trouble describing the formation of giant planets. As mentioned above high massive planet formation is not thought to be possible for orbital radii less than the ice-condensation radius, but high massive planets were discovered down to 0.04 AU. Furthermore, planets with up to 15 jupitermasses were found, and it is not quite clear, whether the standard model can describe the formation of such planets by gas accretion. A high massive protoplanet can open a gap in the orbital region by tidal interaction between the protoplanet and the disk. In detail, gap opening occurs if the gravitational torques excerted by the protoplanet exceed the internal torques due to the viscosity in the disk. However, gap opening decreases the accretion rate since the material of the disk is pushed away from the planet at the planet's orbital region. Therefore it is of interest whether accretion can stop totally or if the resulting decreased accretion rate still leads to high massive planets during the estimated lifetime of the protoplanetary disk (which is about 10e6-10e7 years). Furtheron, it follows from analytical considerations that a embedded protoplanet migrates in the disk as soon as the torques from the inside of the disk and the outside of the disk differ. It is assumed, that giant planets form on radii beyond the snow-line and migrate inwards, where they will be stopped by a unknown mechanism (e.g. by interaction with other protoplanets or magnetohydrodynamical effects). It is even possible that giant planets form out of several smaller gas planets which migrated inwards from radii beyond the snow-line and collided in the inner region of the disk.

 

 
simulations

To gather information about migration rates and the evolution of a protoplanet in the disk, a protoplanetary disk with one or several embedded protoplanet(s) have been simulated using Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics. We have used a undistorted keplerian accretion disk as our initial particle distribution. Most of the simulations were twodimensional, although threedimensional disks were simulated but as no significant differences were found, the threedimensional simulations were skipped due to longer simulation times. For all simulations we used the code v3sph3d, which has been developed by Roland Speith. v3sph3d is a scalar, fortran77 sph code, using a tree algorithm that allows the consideration of the selfgravity of the disk. More information including the source itself can be found at Roland Speith's site.
