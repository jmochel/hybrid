1. Introduction

Welcome to Usenet newsgroup soc.history.what-if!

This is a friendly place where we reject the idea that there are inevitable forces of history that push us towards an inevitable historical outcome. We refute this determinism, and have a lot of fun while doing so, by considering the different possible outcomes of historical events. We explore alternate history, historical what-ifs, allohistory and counterfactuals

We change the past to learn about the consequences, for better or worse, of actions that might have been taken in the past, and to appreciate the role of chance and luck. But we make our changes in the framework of our understanding of the multitude of small changes that are called forces, trends, or developments, as well as the social and technical constraints of an era.

Every action has its consequences!

This Posting Tips FAQ will help you to post to soc.history.what-if and to enjoy your participation in our discussions.

You do not have to be an expert historian to post a what-if to soc.history.what-if. However, make sure you have a some understanding of the historical era of your what-if question before you post it. Sections 1 and 3 of "tips" will help you design your what-if. Section 4 will help you present your what-if in a way that encourages other people to discuss it.

Try and understand the concept of plausibility in history. An alternate history can include dramatic and improbable events and still be plausible.

Plausibility does not equal probability. Rather it attempts to resemble the process of real history. We observe that real history the blend of the many factors. For an alternate history to work it needs to recognize that change results from a wide variety of causes.

Every action has its consequences!

First, history is made by our choices, by our will to act and to persevere. Muhammed, Napoleon, Buddha, St. Paul, Newton, Hitler, Genghis Khan, and many less noticed people changed history by applying their will.

Second, consider chance and luck -- good and bad, such as the message on Japanese military activity that sat on a shelf over the weekend before the Pearl Harbor attack, or Hitler's survival running though the WW I trenches.

Third, consider the progressive accumulation of many small events that we call by names such as trends, forces, processes, and waves.

Plausibility is related to probability in that it captures the summation of all of these results. For example if it took six weeks to sail from Acapulco to Manilla in a Galleon in fair weather and the fastest recorded trip was four weeks, it is not plausible to have an alternate history depend on a usual sailing time of four weeks.

It is plausible to have improbable events, but not an endless series of them. In real history the sequence for "want of of a nail the shoe was lost, for want of a shoe the horse was lost, for want of a horse the battle was lost, for want of a battle the empire was lost" just does not happen. At one stage or antoher someone uses a substitute.

Plausibility is also related to feasibility. A standard example is the Roman steam engine. It is very unlikely that the Romans would have any interest in developing a steam engine. It is of primary value as a labor saving machine. Even if a Roman decided to make one, he could not just go and throw one together at his villa. Our inventive Roman could not make a steam engine without having the ability to do metal fabrication, machining and instrumentation similar to that of 18th century Europe. It took hundreds of years of trial and error to produce these necessary preceding technologies. Consider just one of them, a boring mill to manufacture cylinders. This technology was developed in Europe to make better cannon. So Roman development of a working steam engine--as opposed to a ball that spins by venting steam--would have depended on improving their seige machinery by developing something that fired through tubes. Gunpowder is not essential. The key is precision matching of the tube and projectile diameters.

Plausiblity is also related to the speed at which societies absorb change. We have regular elections and respect the results. This took hundreds of years to be accepted as the way of legitizing power. It would not be plausible to have Ferdinand and Isablella chuck the Inquistion and legitimate their power by popular election.

Plausibility also sums up other elements, which are described in this Posting "Tips" FAQ.

As for alternate history fiction, most of it is not very plausible but we accept it where it creates a persuasive illusion of a world.

Respect our Charter by not discussing adventures in published fictional worlds (see section 2 of "tips"), or revisionist history, such as Holocaust denial, (see section 5, of "tips," which will also help you to avoid other problems.

Finally, "tips" does not attempt to duplicate the "Frequently Asked Questions in soc.history.what-if" maintained and regularly posted to the newsgroup by Craig J Neumeier, (neume001@maroon.tc.umn.edu). Read it.

1. Finding interesting topics

1.01 If your newsreader can sort by thread, scan the list of soc.history.what-if subjects and start with a few that match your interests, whether they are the Normans fail to conquer England or a replay of World War II. Beware, though, there are several cranks around. If you read a post that attacks the poster personally rather than the ideas, ignore it. If you see several objectionable posts from the same person, ignore discussions that involve that person.

1.02 If you find an interesting post but previous posts have expired on your local ISP's server, you can find them at:
        http://www.dejanews.com

1.03 Soc.history.what-if was created by people who wanted a forum to examine real history and see how things might have developed differently if people had made other decisions or had different luck at a historical "point of departure" (POD) This is called alternate history (AH), allohistory, counterfactual history, or uchronia.

1.04 AH topics start with a what-if (WI), usually in the form of a "what-if" question that calls for an answer that explains how history would have been different from real history to cause the change at the POD and explains how the AH would then differ from real history after the POD. The POD can be a battle, an election, the premature death of a key person, the birth of a breakthrough person when during an uneventful period in real history, a scientific discovery, or anything else of historic importance.

2. Published alternate history fiction

Charter prohibits discussion of adventures in published fictional worlds

2.01 Our Charter prohibits discussion of adventures in published fictional worlds, such as Draka or Starship Troopers. The following should help you distinguish what is appropriate for discussion on soc.history.what-if from what should be posted to rec.arts.sf.written:

  • If you want to discuss a published AH story, post to rec.arts.sf.written only. However, there is a limited exemption for speculation about how the history created in the published story would work as an alternate history. This includes discussing history from the individualist perspective of the real world persons that may appear in a published AH, and the characters of the individuals who made history. For example we can take the WI that the South won the US civil war in the manner described in Mr. Turtledove's How Few Remain, and we could discuss how this would affect Abe Lincoln. Our discussion could include the plausibility of Turtledove's speculation that Lincoln's actions would lead Theodore Roosevelt to accuse him Lincoln being a Red Marxist. Similarly we can discuss arguments that How Few Remain misinterpreted Blaine's character and made him take historical actions that he could not have taken in a plausible AH.

  • The authors of successful AH fiction, such as Mr. Turtledove or Mr. Stirling, are sometimes very knowlegeable about the real history of the eras covered by their stories. They choose outcomes that they know are implausible because these are the ones with maximum dramatic impact. For this reason alone their published AH fiction is better discussed in rec.arts.sf.written rather than soc.history.what-if. There is also a Draka fan page at http://www.mindspring.com/~atalbott/index.html

  • There is general consensus that the Draka history is one of the best-developed published alternate histories, which overcomes the fact that it is very improbable in its historical development. However it is implausible mainly because of the improbability that the society could work if it actually came into existence. The uniformity of Draka belief, their high level of civic responsibility, and irresistable slave-training techniques are very unlikely to evolve from the Draka roots as Southern slaveholders. Nor is the docility of the slave culture plausible.

  • If you want to discuss how you enjoyed or detested the story, and fiction specifics, such as the degree of dramatic tension, the amount of "enjoyably nasty stuff," plot resolution by a deus ex machina, "how faithful the movie was to the book, political correctness of the story or movie, the techniques the author uses to develop the character of a fictional person, such as by writing from a first person or third person viewpoint, or colorful description of personal quirks, you should all be post to rec.arts.sf.written.

  • Do not cross-post from soc.history.what-if to rec.arts.sf.written. See section 5 of "tips" for more on cross-posting.

    3. Creating your own alternative history (AH)

    Three basic techniques for creating an AH

    3.01 Set out some circumstances for your proposed what-if and sketch out a few thoughts on what you think might happen if the what-if occured. There are two basic techniques for creating an AH:

  • Reverse-engineer: write backwards. If you conceive of a social arrangenment, such as a British North America east of the Mississipi during the 19th century, all you have to do is figure out the series of events that would have led from your POD, in this case probably no American Revolution, or a failed one, to your British North America of 1876.

  • Or you could postulate a POD and figure out what changes in history would be necessary for a change to occur. For example, the Japanese closed their world to outside influences starting in the 17th century, and this isolation lasted until the Americans forced them to open to foreign trade in the 19th century. What changes in history would be needed for the Japanese to remain open to the world?

  • Consider POD's other than big changes such as who won or lost the usually recognized decisive battle. AH design can be most satisfying if you go further back in history and make a fairly small change. For example the big battle method of keeping the U.S. in the British Empire would be to have the British win the battle of Saratoga. But this is very late in the movement towards independence. It is still likely that as the thirteen colonies recovered from the defeat they would revert, sooner or later, to their common efforts to win independence. The way to have kept the British colonies in the empire would have been to have blocked the initial stages of a single independence movement. An effective POD would make Britain give each colony a separate and independent legislative body by perhaps 1770, with each having its own tax-raising power. This was long before the colonies united to overthrow British rule. This POD would "divide and conquer." Britain would thus have empowered each colony to set its own domestic policy, in the framework of British foreign and imperial policy. As each colony separately developed it would have less and less in common with the other colonies and an independent unified state would be unlikely to develop because each state had a vested interest in maintaining its relative independence.

    Atlas

  • Atlas. Borders change in history. It is very helpful to look a map that shows the names and extent of countries at the ime of your POD. A good historical atlas for this purpose is The Harper Atlas of World History, (William Collins Sons & Co. Ltd., 1987). The format is a series of double page presentations on specific topics with from one to ten simplified maps showing borders, key names, and major changes. A short text explains the maps. A detailed chonology matches the maps. Covers all regions of the world. This is the English translation of Le grand livre de l'histoire du monde; atlas historique, edited by Pierre Vidal-Naquet with text by about 60 persons(Paris; France Loisirs, 1986) ISBN 2.7242.3083.3. Inspired by the historical concept of Braudel, history as the details of all aspects of life.

  • Atlas. The Times Atlas of World History, 4th ed., edited by Geoffrey Barraclough (Times Books, Harper Collins Publishers, London, Hammond Incorporated, Maplewood, NJ;1993) ISBN 0-7230-0534-6. First published in 1978. Any edition is suitable. More general thematic treatment of eras than Harper. An excellent way for you to gain an overall understanding of an era and the changes that can be made in it. My only reservation is that it persists in using Wade-Giles instead of pinyin romanized Chinese names.

  • Atlas. The Anchor Atlas of World History, maps facing chronologies. It is 2 paperback volumes of about 300 pages each. Standard-sized paperbacks; physically easy to handle, compared to a normal atlas, though the maps are quite small. [deserves more comment]

  • Atlas. Colin McEvedy, Penguin Atlas of Ancient History, Penguin Atlas of Medieval History, Penguin Atlas of Modern History, Penguin Atlas of Recent History. Maps are series of overviews of Europe and West Asia, or Europe alone, with a few World maps. A short text explains each map. Focus almost exclusively on Europe and West Asia. Landscape format double size paperbacks; physically easy to handle, compared to a normal atlas. Very handy for quick reference.

    Chronology

  • Chronology. By far the best is a one volume enclopedia last published, AFAIK, in 1972: An Encyclopedia of World History; Ancient, Medieval, and Modern, Chronologically Arranged, compiled and edited by William L. Langer, (Houghton Mifflin, 1948). This is the English language edition of Dr. Karl Ploetz's Auszug aus der alten, mittleren und neueren Geschichte, which went through many editions. This is the best source to use when trying to fit together the bare bones facts of who, what, when, where and why. Few wasted words. In some instances current interpretations of events are different than Langer's. Some ancient dates have been revised. Detail on history outside Western Society and its predecessors is skimpy. But I have yet to find a good supplement to cover China and other non-Western chronological development in detail.

    Technology

  • Technology, ancient. Ancient Inventions by Peter James and Nick Thorpe, (Random House, 1994) ISBN 0-345-36476-7. Easy to understand. Illustrations. For a more scholarly book try R. J. Forbes, Studies in ancient technology, 3rd. ed. (Leiden 1993). Covers Egyptian and Mesopotamian technology as well as the more commonly known Greco-Romans. Useful insights beyond nuts and bolts, such as the surprising failure of greeks and Romans to adopt superior farming techniques that they must have observed in West Asia, which the Arabs later successful used in the Mediterranean region. An AH designer might change this by using a POD based on the idea that the Greeks and Romans they were slave owning societies, which produced a chronic labor surplus that served as a disincentive to seeking labor saving improvements; or a POD to change their attitude of cultural superiority, which made them downplay the value of things that they did not develop themselves.

  • Technology, Chinese. Glance through the following easy to find book before developing strong opinions about ancient Chinese technology and the ability of the Chinese to undertake practical innovation. Robert Temple, with an introduction by Joseph Needham, The Genius of China: 3,000 years of Science, Discovery, and Invention, (Touchstone, Simon and Schuster, 1989) ISBN 0-671-62028-2 ISBN 0-671-67407-2 Pbk Originally published in the U.K. as China: Land of Discovery and Invention, (Patrick Stevens Ltd., 1986). It's also a good source for AH technology devices that look different than the Western tradition. For example there was an ancient Chinese system of measurements -- length, width, volume -- based on the musical pitches of bells. By contrast the modern metric system began with the Englightenment idea of replacing the measure of the distance between the kings nose and fingertip (the yard) with a crude measure of of the circumferance of world, divided by a number that would yield an answer about the same length as the yard. Western measures displaced the Chinese measures based on harmonic sounds. But when you think about it, physics and engineering based on harmonic measures could have progressed directly to measuring distance by the frequency of light waves, as we do now, and could have skipped much of the instrumentation based development of Western measurement.

    Ideas

  • Ideas. Disease. Plagues and Peoples by William H. McNeill. A top historian's readable overview of disease and history. How plagues hit the late Roman Empire, stalled Alexander's invasion of India, required cities to rely on migration from rural areas to maintain their populations. For a current assessment of plagues by a biology professor see Yellow Fever, Black Goddess: The Coevolution of People and Plagues, by Christopher Wills (Addison-Wesley Pub Co, 1997). Both McNeill and Wills argue that evolution will uniformly favor benign pathogens over the long term. Because parasites need hosts to survive, virulent pathogens would be at an evolutionary disadvantage. Pathogens, such as Yersinia pestis, the bacterium that causes bubonic plague, survive only in specialized conditions and are highly vulnerable to things that disturb its universe, which explains why plague outbreaks do not last. But there is a caveat for McNeill and Wills. They limit their definition of plague to something that eventually subsides. The most dramatic examples are the pandemics such as the Black Death of the 6th, 14th and 17th centuries and the Influenza of 1918. But there are diseases that kill in large numbers for millennia. A contrary view is that there are perpetual plagues where the particulars of transmission favor both high virulence and high frequency of infection. For example areas where mosquito density is perpetually high, enable Plasmodium falciparum malaria to kill many people, year after year. The AH designer should consider the effects of these perpetual plagues diseases as well as sporatic ones. Such perpetual plagues blocked Western exploitation of Africa for centuries until modern medicine and provided some protection. For views that contrast with McNeil and Wills see a book by another biology professor, Paul Ewald, Evolution of Infectious Disease (Oxford University Press, 1994).

  • Ideas. The Measure of Reality: Quantification and Western Society, 1250-1600, Alfred W. Crosby (1997). ISBN 0-521-55427-6 ISBN 0-521-63990-5 Pbk. A thesis that the West's great distinction was to meld mathematics and measurement into a mindset -- to use quantification to understand everything material within time and space; to measure reality. Argues that quantification explains how the West leaped from a sub-Euclidean legacy to the universe Galileo describes, to a culture that spawned navigation, astronomy, cartography, commercial and banking procedures and practical and theoretical mathematics. This change spread through the psyche--mentalité--of the West. It became the way of thinking of the world that gave the West decisively important administrative, commercial, industrial and military skills not developed in other Societies. This suggests AH's in which one of the other major Societies, such as Islam or China, evolve the quantification mentalité before the West, and thereby modernize and dominate the world.

  • Ideas. The Colonizer's Model of the World, J.M. Blaut. A thesis that that the rise of Europe to modernity and world dominance is the result of is access to the New World, which unbalanced the Eurasian economic system in favour of Europe, rather than due to some unique European quality of race, environment, culture, mind, or spirit, and that progress for the rest of the world results from the diffusion of European civilization. Blaut argues the diffusionist doctrine is the world model constructed by Europeans to explain, justify, and assist their colonial expansion. This suggests AH's in which one of the other major Societies, such as Islam or China, discover and exploit the Americas before the Europeans. Use Blaut's thesis with caution. European sea dominance came centuries before industrial take-off and the economic dominance of Western Society. Blaut fails to prove that they were causally related.

  • Ideas. Annales historians, Braudel, Darnton, Ladurie, Schama. Since about 1929 Annales historians have rejected both classical political history, which assumes common people live in history but do not make history and that political ideas govern economic behavior; and the Procrustean bed of any determinist big theory, such as Marx. A synthesis of styles and specialities, focused on data from primary documents, closely checked rather than accepted as prima facie true and complete. Annalists search for long term historical dynamics, without assuming it must be economic interest, religious ideology, or dynastic ambitions of royal dynasties. I highly recommend many of the Annales books to AH researchers for their fascinating detail and high quality writing. The top Annales historian was Fernand Braudel, The Mediterranean and Civilization and Capitalism 15th-18th Century. Braudel wrote in French and was mostly ignored in English speaking countries until his books were translated into English.

  • Ideas. Ecology. AH design can benefit from biological and ecological perspectives on human history which, among other things offer recurrent motifs such as "the irrigation system collapsed." Clive Ponting, Green History of the World. Humans have been destroying the environment since prehistoric times. Many examples of destruction for AH designers to change. A simple formula for disaster: successful Societies produce surplus food which allows the rise of culture, civil and military hierarchies. These institutions vastly increase demands on the ecosystem for food and energy that outstrip the local ecosystem's ability to sustain. What follows is an inevitable downward cycle of diminishing returns resulting from over-irrigation of lands, leading to rising of water tables and the salinization of the land and a consequent fall in the production of the agricultural output leading to eventual shortfalls in surpluses, starvation and the demise of the culture. Other examples: deforestation, extermination of species.

  • Ideas. Environmental determinism, biogeography. Jared Diamond, Guns, Germs and Steel, (W.W. Norton & Co, 1997, ISBN 0-224-03809-5). Western Society defeated its competitors because geography gave it diverse flora and fauna. Eurasia had more types of plants and large animals to domesticate than other regions. Ambitious broad brush human history since the Ice Age in the context of geography, demography, and ecological happenstance. Eurasia has geography better than other regions for to farming, domestication of animals, and information flow. It developed populous cultures with complex government and communication -- and increased resistance to disease. Societies that gained a head start in producing food would overpower preliterate ones. Domestication of animals made Eurasians resistant to these germs that they later spread in epidemic proportions in their voyagesof discovery. Diamonds's thesis is a "positive feedback loop" from farming, domestication, population density, to innovation. Why did peoples of some regions fail to domesticate plants? Why were most big wild mammal species never domesticated? Why did food production spread at different rates on different continents? The final chapter, "The Future of History as a Science," is almost an AH factory. It argues that students of humanity have examples and "natural experiments" to make comparisons. For example, after the 15th century fragmented Europe harnessed competitive innovation in ways that over-unified China did not. Christendom adopted the wheel, the key machine element, while the Islamic Society discarded it

  • Ideas. Evolution and "biological determinism" This category of books provides many unexpected examples and illustrations that you can adapt to explain AH changes. Jared Diamond, The Rise and Fall of the Third Chimpanzee (ISBN 0 09 991380 1). But how do we reconcile the idea of evolution by natural selection relentlessly favoring self-interest, for example Dawkins, The Selfish Gene, with the complex societies developed by human beings and the abundance of human cooperative spirit? For an answer read Matt Ridley, The Origins of Virtue: Human Instincts and the Evolution of Cooperation, (Viking Penguin, 1997, ISBN 0670874493). Matt Ridley summarizes his book. It "is about the billion-year coagulation of our genes into cooperative teams, the million-year coagulation of our ancestors into cooperative societies, and the thousand-year coagulation of ideas about society and its origins." He suggests a new paradox: "Our minds have been built by selfish genes, but they have been built to be social, trustworthy and cooperative." The AH designer will find inspiration in examples of societies built upon voluntary exchange of goods, information, fortune and power between free individuals -- trust, mutuality and reciprocity -- before authoritarian government suppressed the arrangements. Merchant guilds. Merchant communities in North Italy (which was spared the strong monarchies and godfathers of the South). Trade precedes law. Modern commercial law was invented and enforced by merchants to facilitate trade in the 11th century. Merchants in a foreign country had no recourse to their prince if cheated and no confidence that the same standards applied there as at home. Merchants voluntarily produced, voluntarily adjudicated and voluntarily enforced the lex mercatoria. It evolved. Natural selection allowed good customs and ways of settling disputes to drive out inferior ones. By the 12th century it provided substantial protection. But the adapability of the system was lost as goverments increased their power and froze the arrangements into laws enforced by the state. Merchants have made repeated attempts to bypass the congested courts by commercial arbitration. The collapse of community spirit in the last few decades, and the erosion of civic virtue, is caused not by the the spread and encouragement of greed but by the spread of Levitathan, the powerful state that makes no bargain with the citizen to take joint responsibility for civic order, engenders in the citizen no obligation, duty or pride, and imposes obedience instead. Thus the citizen, treated as an naughty child, behaves like one. Examples of where authority replaced reciprocity and the sense of community failed. Ineffective welfare state based on condensation replaced effective community institutions. Material improvement is the result of new technology, not government. Socially the deterioration is marked "for St Augustine the source of social order lay in the teachings of Christ. For Hobbes it lay in the sovereign. For Lenin it lay in the party. They were all wrong. The roots of social order are in our heads, where we possess the instincitve capacities for creating not a perfectly harmonious and virtuous society, but a better one than we have at present." The AH designer should recognize that the medieval vassal and the industrial age factory worker had no freedom to build a society based on trust and reciprocity between equals in a Society dominated by the heirarchical authority of prince and church, aristocratic or industrial system. But there are many other opportunities to apply the thesis that government does not impose virtue on inherently evil people, which is the thesis of Hobbes' Leviathan and contemporary liberals. "We are not so nasty that they need to be tamed by intrusive government, nor so nice that intrusive government does not bring out the worst in us, both as its employees and as its clients." History provides many POD's where a society could have developed alternatives to a "government that dictates the minutist details of life and squats like a giant flea upon the back of the nation."

  • Ideas. Evolution and "biological determinism" Other views: Steve Jones, The Language of the Genes (ISBN 0 00 654676 5) and In the Blood (ISBN 0 00 255511 5).

  • Ideas. The Nine Nations of North America, Joel Garreau. A journalist argues that North America is nine "nations" based on culture and economics. These offer a set of possible alternatives to real world boundaries. Garreau's nations are: New England, Quebec, The Foundry (Industrial MidWest, PA, NY, NJ, etc.), The Breadbasket (Great Plains), The Empty Quarter (Rocky Mountains, Great Basin, most of Alaska and non-Pacific Western Canada), Ecotopia (Pacific Northwest, sometimes called Cascadia), Mexamerica (Mexico, South Texas and California), Dixie (Deep South), The Islands (South Florida, the Caribbean, and the north coast of South America).

  • Ideas. Connections and The Day the Universe Changed, James Burke. Companion books to BBC TV series. Burke talking about unexpected interrelations of technologies, economics, history, etc. Burke is a good source of AH ideas but should be read with the caveat that many of his linkages are very forced. His working technique was to find two seemingly unrelated events and then dig until he found some way to link them. Great drama but not a plausible causal progression.

  • Ideas. Indian Givers : How the Indians of the Americas Transformed the World, Jack Weatherford (1990 ?). Discovery of the New World affected Europe through increasing the supply of gold and silver, adding new food crops and medicines, inspiring democracy. Note particularly the AH possibilities in the chapter on Cahokia and the Mississipians. Read with the caveat that it has a PC bias. For example it overates the American Indian contribution to American democracy. The Iroquois constitution is a pale shadow of the inspiration the US Founders took from of ancient Greece and Rome.

  • Don't discount books written primarily for juvenile readers. They can help clarify complex issues. For example the Kingfisher Illustrated History of the World: 40,000 BC to the Present Day, Charlotte Evans, general editor (New York: Kingfisher Books, 1993). ISBN 1856978621. Fairly thorough, with plenty of maps and a timeline with many POD possibilities.

  • Books recommended to me that I have not evaluated. You may find them useful. Peter Brown, The Body and Society. Intellectual and social history of the Antique world; and The Rise of Western Christendom, (much broader in scope than the title suggests).
    Edward Hyams - Soil and Civilization, John Perlin - A Forest Journey. World histories from the perspective of soil and tree use respectively.

    Plausibility, probability, the unexpected, luck and chance

    3.03 Plausibility, probability, the unexpected, luck and chance.

  • You should do some sort of reality check on your POD and AH. It is not plausible to propose that the Roman Empire survives almost unchanged to the present day, with the exception that the Imperial guards use machine guns and some totalitarian techniques.

  • "Plausible" does not mean just the most "probable" outcome of a change in history. You can write a plausible AH showing major changes caused by the actions of a person of improbable origin. This has happened in real history. Consider Napoleon, a Corsican who might not even have been French if there had been slight changes in history a few years before he was born.

  • To use a person who did not exist in real history to do improbable things, all you need to do is check that the cultural, socio-political and technological circumstances were so that such a person could make the changes. Indeed, for a plausible AH extending more than a few years beyond the POD, you should, like real history, include some unexpected world-altering individuals, and develop the socio-political environment that enables their actions to be believable.

  • Also, don't forget the role of chance and luck in real history. A plausible AH will include some of these elements. For example a storm blows an attack fleet off course, a major religious leader falls down the stairs and dies before delivering the word of God. Real history also has the message on Japanese military activity that sat on a shelf over the weekend before the Pearl Harbor attack, and Hitler's survival running though the WW I trenches.

    Plausibility checklist

    3.04 Checking the plausibility of your AH is mostly common sense. However you might want to think about a few specific checks on it. These could include:

  • An attempt to capture the socio-political and technological evolution of the era.

  • Avoiding extremes that contradict the socio-political and technological realities of the era. For example, an empire that lasts virtually forever, like year 2000 Rome, a "super superior empire" like the ficitional Draka who make no mistakes (stirring fiction but not very plausible), or a people rising from the ashes of defeat to dominate the world. If your AH claims any of these, you are going to have a lot of work to create a plausible explanation. Similarly, you can create a good looking AH empire or country on the map. But to make it plausible, you need to apply the same factors that made it possible for empires and countries to exist in real history.

  • Avoiding an isolated dramatic event, in isolation from socio-political and technological changes, as the means of creating major changes. For example Napoleon conquered much of Europe. Your AH would be implausible if you put a Napoleon syle Emperor in France 50 years earlier and said he maide these conquests. The AH would lack the essential context of revolutionary popular enthusiasm and changes such as the Levee en Masse.

  • Avoiding the "easy erase" trap of removing something from real history that was the natural result of socio-political and technological developments. For example having an empire collapse without apparent reason or not forming, despite the presence of factors that make this by far the easiest course of action for people to take.

  • Avoiding instant technology which is widely adopted despite incompatable social context. For example having Roman steam engines without preparing the way with metallugry and machining skills that need to preceed the device, and without social changes to to the cheap labor market that made the Romans uniterested in labor saving technology.

  • Recognizing that if your AH covers more than a few years after your POD there will be a progressive accumulation of changes in peoples beliefs, socio-political and economic activities, and technology.

  • Recognizing that if you use historical personalities in your AH, they are plausible if they were born before the POD, but increasingly implausible as the time increases between the POD and their birth, with the implausibility increasing as their ancestors are directly related to the changes caused by the POD. In fiction we can use Nixon as a car salesman as a joke, for an AH of British America, but its not plausible at all.

  • Recognizing that we can discuss AH most clearly when we look at just a few years after a POD. When we go beyond that we have no idea what is plausible, except that it makes some logical sense to see AH as changed by a "ripple effect" of accumulated micro changes after the POD. A POD in modern high-communication society will propagate very rapidly. But the rate slows as we look further back in history, until we reach the pre-electronic world where local cultures were mainly isolated from the outside.

  • Recognizing when the "ripple effect" is strong. This would include eras when the composition of the elite population has direct consequences on politics. History is full of wars caused by dynastic disputes, and elites elbowing for more power. These events are most sensitive to microchange effects. During the pre-modern era of Latin Christiandom claimants to dynastic titles fought wars to their support their claims. The failure to have a male child could plunge society into war and lead to major shifts in political control. Its fun to write these AH's but they can soon become very complex. After the 17th century there was a gradual shift to sovereignty based on support of the population, and wars became more closely related to national interests, which reduced this "ripple effect."

  • Applying a rational concept of historical change. AFAIK nobody has ever been to an alternate world. We can make all sorts of speculation about a "ripple effect" producing explosive changes, because of chaos theory etc. But the only speculations of any possible value to us are those that are bounded by rationality. When we look at real history we observe that most changes are incremental rather than explosive, and can be explained by the effects of the interaction of many individual decisions. Human nature makes most people reasonably predictable in their behavior. They earn a living, have children etc. Personalities rarely radically transform. Napoleon had a personality that needed to gamble all on the single chance and was compelled to provide kingships for his relatives even when this sabotaged public support for the new governments. If we looked at him after a POD he would still have this personality, rather than a different one, such as where he transformed into a cautious conserver of his gains who built popular support for new kings with local roots. Technology permits some things at a particular time but not others. An ancient Roman tinkerer could not invent a steam engine one summer in the back room of a villa and revolutionize Roman industry.

    What is plausible technological change?

    3.11 Technological change, as mentioned above, should be plausible. However, you can do a plausible AH where science and technology differ considerably from the real world. Bear in mind that changes in science and technology are very complex. Nothing happens in isolation. A change in science or technology at a POD requires changes before the POD that make it possible for the change to work. For example you can't introduce a steam engine without first showing a history of preceding technological development in metal fabrication, machining and instrumentation. In addition to showing the development of the knowledge required to make a science or technology change, your AH should deal with the natural consequences of the change, including further discoveries and applications. If a society invents and adopts steam engines, power source development will not stop there, even if it does not follow the pattern of real history. Here are some tips to help your technological arguments.

  • Similar societies have about the same rate of technological change. A single discovery, no matter how fundamental, will usually fail to transform a the long-term development of a society. A dramatic change in a society's rate of technological development needs a dramatic change in society itself. You can achieve this by designing an AH where the working population drops and there is a demand for labor saving machines, as it did in Europe in the 14th century. Or you can replace a restrictive government that discourages entrepreneurialism and science, such as inquisitorial Spain, with a one that encourages dynamic centers of learning, as occurred in Holland in the 17th century. Or you can inject new wealth into a society ready to innovate to make profits, as occurred in England in the 18th century.

  • However, it is much easier to design an AH to retard rather than advance the rate of technological change. The bulk of technology relies on previous discoveries being completed. A big speedup of a specific technology will hit barriers as it runs beyond the prerequisite components that need to be developed by other technologies. In real history many "crash projects" have failed because they have run beyond available inputs. The project members then have to develop an incredible range of related technologies before they can advance in the project. A project that attempts to create a wide range of technologies quickly reaches the point of diminishing returns fairly quickly. Consider the simple example of the person in a Roman villa inventing a steam engine. They have to develop the full range of technologies for making high quality steel, boring cylinders (a technology developed in real history to bore cannons), making regulatory controls (a technology developed by scientists in real history ] to develop instrumentation to measure the real world) etc. The problem of a technology before its time is becomes worse as the technologies become more sophisticated.

  • As returns diminish, wastage increases. A society with a huge increase in its research budget must be able to afford to throw away a lot of money. An unavoidable economic consequence of major crash research programs (those that consume a significant share of Gross National Product, a measure of economic wealth) as demonstrated by military research during the Cold War, is that they drain money from the economy, and lower economic growth.

  • It's difficult to design an AH that plausibly "deletes" a technology entirely is hard. It is rarely sufficient to remove the person credited with a discovery. Except in ancient times, when there was little technological change anyway, discoveries are usually the result of the work of many people. Also, most of the major scientific and technological discoveries were the evident goals to people who had observed previous development. The achievement of most discoveries is by perseverance in seeking to reach these goals. As time after the previous development increases, the nature of the next discovery becomes more and more obvious. Your AH can delay discoveries, but the longer you delay them, the more likely the it is that some researcher will make the breakthrough.

  • Many technologies have alternatives. This is particularly so for core technologies such as power generation, electronics, weapons, fuels, and transport and propulsion systems for land, air, sea and space. For example, in real history petroleum replaced coal as the major fuel because it was more efficient and less costly. If the oil industry had been delayed we would have developed alternatives, such as alcohol fueled engines. They have lower performance but are still useful enough for to develop an early 20th century level auto industry. As petroleum was developed as a source for gasoline, it would displace alcohol fueled engines. A key point is that the fact that a certain technology dominates usage in real history usually means that the alternatives are more costly and less efficient, rather than non-existent.

  • Wishful thinking does not make a feasible technology. An AH based on a theoretically possible technology can fail if it is not practically feasible. There are many "maybe" technologies, such as Maglev trains, turbine autos, and dynasoar space planes, that were never seriously implemented in real history because they would worked poorer and were more costly than their optimistic supporters predicted, or would take an excessive development time and money before the problems could be solved and they could be incorporated into working real-world systems. Dream technology for space travel or computers that would have made an AH better than the real world is particularly suspect. Before you incorporate dream technology in your AH, consider the credibility of its advocates. Often they have an axe to grind with the owners of the existing technology and have a vested interest in the dream technology. Or they are simply impractical dreamers who ignore the difficulties inherent in the technology. The technologies used in the real world, whether today or 2000 years ago, are the ones which met the practical needs of society. When you find a technology that was not used in real history, find the reason why it was not used. If you want to introduce the technology, design your AH so that reason does not exist. For example if you want a Roman steam engine, you will have to create demand for labor saving machinery. You would have to transform Roman society from one of cheap labor to one of labor shortage. An initial step would be to abolish slavery. However this is easier said than done, and you would also need to make a lot of other social changes.

    Ancient technolology

    3.12 Ancient technolology. An early, and vivid fictional AH proposed that as an alternative to the collapse of the Roman Empire and the onset of the "Dark Ages" a time-traveller would arrive in Rome and introduced twentieth century technology. This was L. Sprague de Camp's 1939 novel, Lest Darkness Fall. The hero introduces one invention after another, transforms Rome and prevents the Fall. But you can make your what-if operate within the realm of plausibility. Consider the classic AH of the Roman steam engine:

  • The Romans were good at technological development, by taking existing basic concepts and improving them where it was sufficiently desired and cost-effective. However they were not technical innovators.

  • Would the Romans be interested in developing a steam engine. It is of primary value as a labor saving machine. But the romans had a glut of slaves and Plebs. Consider cost-effectivness. Supply and demand meant that there was nothing to be gained by developing machinery for anything except toys and spectacles (temple doors etc). The Romans were practical to the extreme and limited R&D to where they needed it--cement, good plumbing (great baths are practical from the point of view of the user).

  • Until the mid-19th century, with the advances in Chemistry, technology has developed independently of science. Practical measurements and iterative tests--often extremely unsafe--were used instead of mathematical modelling to develop things such as early cannon and steam engines. Check out how, for example, James Watt perfected the steam engine (1775).

  • The geometry that the Romans used in their construction was sufficient for this project. They knew about Euclid's "Elements" of geometry and there was no shortage of Greek teachers to explain the finer points.

  • However, it would have been beyond Roman ability to make a working steam engine until they developed a boring mill to manufacture cylinders. This technology was developed in Europe to make better cannon. So Roman development of a working steam engine--as opposed to a Hero's ball that spins by venting steam--would have depended on improving their seige machinery by developing something that fired through tubes. Gunpowder is not essential. The key is precision matching of the tube and projectile diameters. Seems like there is a possibility here for a good AH!

  • The creative AH designer might find another reason for the Romans to develop a boring mill than to make cannon.

  • Alternatively, the AH designer might have the Romans stick to rotation, and develop a steam turbine. However, this would have its own requirements: including balancing and reliable, effective bearings.

  • Either way, they'd have to perfect fairly high-quality steel.

  • Given several centuries the Romans could solve these problems. But the AH designer must motivate the Romans to do so by making labor much more expensive relative to machinery.

    Early Modern Western science and technology

    3.14 Early Modern Western science and technology. My impression has been for some time that quantification is the key to understanding why the West developed modern science and technology while neither Islam nor China did. I note in this regard a recent book by Alfred W. Crosby, The Measure of Reality: Quantification and Western Society, 1250-1600 (see my mini review in section 3.02) argues along somewhat similar lines.

    3.15 In particular I argue that analytic geometry, and instrumentation are key factors in Europe's scientific revolution. First, analytic geometry:

  • As early as the third century A.D. the Chinese developed geometrical propositions in algebraic form and described geometrical figures by algebraic equations. The Muslims acquired these techniques when al-Khwarizmi was the Kalif's ambassador to Khazaria.

  • But neither the Chinese nor the Muslims went on to develop analytic geometry, which can reference every geometical object and every geometric operation. This was first done by Pierre Fermat and Rene Descartes in the 17th century, when Western scientists began applying mathematics to every aspect of the physical world.

    • 3.16 As for instrumentation and mathematics, consider a few examples:

  • Huygens patented the first pendulum clock in 1656. This gave the first reasonably accuracte time measurements. The pendulum clock was based on Huygens theory of evolutes, specifically the cycloid. The mathematical theory and the mechanical applications developed in parallel. Other curves also had a "mechanical" equivalent, such as the tractrix -- the curve traced by one end of a rod when the other end is dragged along a line perpendicular to the initial position of the rod. Mathematics also solved the problems of developing optics.
  • A good example of the 17th century quantification of natural phenomena is the development of thermometers and the mathematical understanding of the expansion of air as its heat increases.

  • No other culture independently developed scientific instrumentation to match what was developed in Europe from the 17th century onwards, or made mathematical calculations with detailed quantification of of temperatures, pressures, chemical reactions, produced by scientific hypothesis, observation and experiment.

    • 3.17 My quantification and instrumentaion argument for the Western scientific revolution requires a few important qualifications:

  • The 17th century scientific revolution consisted of work by people attempting a variety of goals. Some were pure alchemy and magic, rather than the observation of and experimention on the real world that we recognize as "science." Some of the best scientific results were byproducts of projects that did not have "scientific" goals.

  • Part of the 17th century scientific revolution was experimental science, part was abstract scientific method, part was the systematic application of instrumentation techniques (optics, measuring devices). Sometimes they came together in the quantification and sometimes they developed independently.

  • Sociological, religious, economic and other non-technical reasons were as important as the sequence of technical developments, which I characterized as the "mathematization of science," in causing the 17th century scientific revolution. None of the explanations can be extracted from the complex of causes.

    • 3.18 Some of our most interesting what-ifs deal with postulated failure of the European scientific revolution and its possible development by Islamic or Chinese scientists.

    The English Industrial Revolution

    3.19. The English Industrial Revolution. The 17th century scientific revolution was not the key factor leading to the 18th century industrial revolution in England, which developed mainly from hands on "technics" -- techniques learned by trial and error. The matching of the results of the 17th century scientific revolution with the industrial revolution came initially in the application of the scientific instrument making techniques to machines that required pressure and temperature regulator and monitoring devices. Only later stages of the industrial revolution required extensive use of the results of the 17th century scientific revolution. This occured, for example, in the chemistry of synthethic dyes in the second half of the 19th century.

    Organize your presentation

    4.05 Organize your presentation:

  • Begin with a sentence that expands your Subject line enough so that readers understand what you are trying to do.

  • Explain what actually happened in real history. This is unnecessary for events that are very well known such as dropping the Atomic Bomb on Hiroshima in 1945.

  • Explain what might have happened. This is your POD and what made it happen differently than real history. Don't just ask a question, such as "what if Germany won WW I?" without giving some hint as to how and why this happened. A good WI will give a plausible explanation, such as how the Germans were able to break through in their 1914 advance to Paris. Do some research and think before posting your WI. No explanation = rude and lazy.

  • What are the immediate results of your POD. Don't just ask a question such as "what if Caesar stayed home on the Ides March and was not assassinated?" without giving some hint as as to what what you think would happen. Do some research and think before posting your WI. A whatif with no explanation tells people that you are rude and lazy.

  • What are the short-term results. This starts your timeline after your POD. Don't just list a series of events.Try to explain why you consider each event will occur, or at least some historical expanation of why things will move in this direction.

  • What are the consequences for the next year or so.

  • What are the longer term consequences.

  • You can offer a single AH or suggest several possibilities.

  • These points are merely to help you organize your thoughts. You do not have to write them down or use all of them. Some historical events are so well known that an explanation of real history is unnecessary. But you will get more of a response if you err on the side of explaining enough real world history around your POD and AH to allow readers to understand the difference from real history and comment without having to duplicate your research. Of course, we hope that they will check their facts, or be already knowlegeable about the era and event, before they jump in with a detailed critique.

    Do not encourage "Holocaust deniers" to argue their views

    5.07 Understand how to deal with historical truth as an issue, and prevent Nazis who infest Usenet from making you a sucker:

  • Alt.revisionism, rather than soc.history.what-if, is the place for debates on the truth of the commonly accepted historical record. Our Charter enables us to discuss alternative histories in which events differ from the known historical record, rather than denials of the truth of the commonly accepted historical record, such as the type known as "Holocaust denial."
  • Alternate history operates on the assumption that the commonly accepted historical record is substantially accurate.

  • Secret history is dedicated to revising the commonly accepted historical record. It is incompatible with alternate history. In partiular we have seen many posts by a few persons who deny that the Holocaust happened, or that a faulty historical record over reports it. In this insance, secret history accepts an alternate history as the truth rather than the commonly accepted historical record. This discussion is suitable for alt.revisionism but not for soc.history.what-if.

  • However, the soc.history.what-if Charter does allow discussion of "what if the Holocaust had never happened." Anyone posing this WI should beware of the infestation of Holocaust deniers that it will pull out of the woodwork. Similarly discussion of WI's in which a post WW II Nazi Germania managed to suppress knowledge of the Holocaust (as in the AH postulated in the novel Fatherland, will probably encourage the fans of secret history to reinvade soc.history.what-if.

  • To sum up, a valid AH for soc.history.what-if cannot claim that events which demonstrably happened in real history, such as the Holocaust, didn't happen in real history. But a valid AH for soc.history.what-if may ask how the world would have been had the Holocaust, or other event denied by believers in secret history, had not happened.

    • Return to start

    Do not engage in "fanish" discussion of published AH fiction

    5.08 Do not engage in "fanish" discussion of published AH fiction:

  • If you are interested in adventures in published fictional worlds, role playing games and sci-fi and other fantastic entertainments, such as "1984 vs Draka" or "President RELee vs the Borgyou" can pursue your interest in alt.worldsaway, rec.games.frp.misc, rec.arts.sf.written, alt.fan.[authorsname], or at web sites dedicated to the published fiction of individual authors such as Steve Stirling's Draka at
    http://www.mindspring.com/~atalbott/index.html

  • Unfortunately there is no specific alternate history rpg newsgroup. I would encourage rpg fans to create alt.games.frp.alt-hist.

  • If you are a fan of the excellent GURPS Alternate Earths please post on rec.games.frp.gurps.

  • As noted above our Charter does not prohibit discussion of historical possibilities merely because they have been used in published fiction. Our charter permits speculation about how the history created in the published story would work as an alternate history. For example Harry Turtledove's published AH stories, the Agent of Byzantium series, is based on the interesting POD of Muhammed becoming a Christian and develops an AH that envisions a flourishing Byzantine Empire. But other published AH's are extremely improbabile, which means that most discussion of them would be best done on rec.arts.sf.written.

  • The Charter also allows discussion of how well the possibilites of the AH are realized in the published fiction. But the Charter draws the line at discussion of the cardboard nature of the characters, their limited viewpoint, dramatic tension or other aspects of the author's writing technique such as "enjoyably nasty stuff," or "easy to pull a deus ex machina out of a hat," or "[author's name] ended up unreadable." (Quotes from the "1984 Vs. The Draka" thread.)

    Return to start

    Do not engage in discussion of future history

    5.09 Do not engage in discussion of future history:

  • Note that this only applies to past history. Speculations about future history should go to alt.history.future.

    Do not post binaries

    5.10 Use binaries appropriately:

  • You may reach the stage where you have a great picture or map to add life to your WI.

  • But do not post binaries in soc.history.what-if. They belong only in the hierarchies explicitly intended for them. The reason for this is to allow news servers with limited resources to avoid these (very expensive) postings. You can post your binary to to a binary newsgroup or put it on a web site and post a reference in soc.history.what-if.

    6. Text of the charter of soc.history.what-if

    6.01 The following is the text of the charter of soc.history.what-if as approved by vote reported on May 16, 1995:

    --[start quote]------------------------------------------------

    CHARTER

    The soc.history.what-if newsgroup will be open to discussion of alternate history. This is "what-ifs" regarding specific historical events.

    Specifically, but not exclusively:

  • Historical events - what could have happened if they had been different?
  • How could this have happened differently (i.e. discussion of how the divergence could have occurred, not of what its consequences would be.)

    • Note: the following topics are not to be discussed:

  • Revisionism regarding the Holocaust or Turkish/Armenian massacres (post to alt.revisionism). "What if the Holocaust had not happened?" is a legitimate question
  • Future history - "What if the President were assassinated tomorrow?"
  • Alternate history in fictional worlds - "What if Luke had failed to destroy the Death Star?"

    • --[end quote]------------------------------------------------

    There is no charter for alt.history.what-if, the newsgroup that according to the CFV was supposed to disappear. The rationale circulated with the CFV is as follows:

    --[start quote]------------------------------------------------

    RATIONALE

    alt.history.what-if is a thriving newsgroup. It has a reasonable posting level, a well-maintained FAQ (thank you, Robert Schmunk), and a good signal-to-noise level (no ad hominems, only a few flames before a recent flamewar that seems to have stayed in one thread, and is dying).

    But it's on alt.*. Lots of people can't get alt.*. (Lots of .ac.uk sites for a start). So I propose we move it to soc.*

    There are several other soc.history.* groups, and it will fit well into that hierarchy.

    If this CFV passes, a proposal will be made for alt.history.what-if to be rmgrouped. I appreciate that alt.* groups never truly die, but I hope that it will disappear from most sites.

    --[end quote]-------------------------------------------------

    The following is quoted from the announcement of the vote results:

    --[start quote]------------------------------------------------

    There were 166 YES votes and 45 NO votes, for a total of 211 valid votes. There were 3 abstains and 4 invalid ballots.

    For group passage, YES votes must be at least 2/3 of all valid (YES and NO) votes. There also must be at least 100 more YES votes than NO votes.

    --[end quote]-------------------------------------------------
